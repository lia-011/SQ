HISTORY OF PEKAN KUNDASANG

Historically, Pekan Kundasang was home to indigenous Dusun and Kadazan communities who engaged in farming. During World War II, it became part of the tragic Sandakan Death Marches, where Allied POWs were forced to march through the area, leading to the deaths of many prisoners. Post-war, the region developed into an agricultural hub, particularly for vegetables, thanks to its favorable climate. Today, Kundasang is a popular tourist destination, serving as a gateway to Mount Kinabalu and housing memorials such as the Kundasang War Memorial Park to honor the POWs, while its agricultural and tourism sectors continue to shape its identity.

Early Settlement

Pekan Kundasang began as a small settlement for the Dusun community, who traded crops and goods in the area. Its strategic location near Mount Kinabalu made it a natural gathering and trading point.

Colonial Era

During British rule, the town remained a quiet village but benefited from improved infrastructure that connected it to larger towns, enhancing trade and accessibility.

World War II Impact

Pekan Kundasang played a minor but significant role during the Sandakan Death March as nearby areas witnessed the atrocities of war. The war memorial later became a landmark close to the town.

Modern Growth

Over time, Pekan Kundasang evolved into a bustling center for local produce, with its market becoming a popular stop for travelers. Its development was further boosted by tourism tied to Mount Kinabalu and nearby attractions.
